1.0 Executive Summary

This document outlines a series of profound procedural, legal, and ethical failures in the management of the Swanage Shore Road public consultation. It serves as a formal complaint and a pre-action letter demanding immediate corrective action from Swanage Town Council (STC), its agent the Dorset Coast Forum (DCF), and their technology provider, Granicus Inc.

The central issue is the mid-survey implementation of a high-friction, discriminatory CAPTCHA, a decision made in direct response to a legitimate public engagement campaign by the North Swanage Traffic Concern Group (NSTCG). We are in receipt of written confirmation from DCF that this action was taken on the specific recommendation of Granicus.

This unilateral action corrupted the democratic process by:

  • Violating UK Law: Breaching the Equality Act 2010 by creating a discriminatory barrier for older residents and those with disabilities, and breaching UK GDPR by processing data unfairly and non-transparently.
  • Contaminating the Dataset: Creating two different cohorts of respondents and systematically biasing the results, rendering the entire dataset statistically invalid and unusable for public policy.
  • Failing Democratic Standards: Violating the established common law Gunning Principles for a fair and lawful public consultation.

This document details the chain of failure across all three organisations and serves as notice that unless the demanded corrective actions are taken within the specified timeline, the NSTCG will proceed with a coordinated series of escalations, including formal complaints to the ICO, the LGO, and the EHRC, a claim for Judicial Review at the High Court, and a public campaign to expose this matter to the media, Parliament, and Granicus's corporate investors.

2.1 The Swanage Shore Road Consultation

The Swanage Shore Road project is a matter of significant public interest. To engage the community, Swanage Town Council, through its agent the Dorset Coast Forum, launched a public consultation survey hosted on the "Engagement HQ" platform, provided by Granicus Inc. The stated purpose was to gather public opinion to inform future policy.

2.2 The Parties Involved: Client, Agent, and Provider

  • Swanage Town Council (STC): The "Client." The elected public body with ultimate responsibility for ensuring a lawful, fair, and accountable consultation process on behalf of its constituents.
  • Dorset Coast Forum (DCF): The "Agent." The project manager contracted by STC to manage the day-to-day operations of the consultation.
  • Granicus Inc.: The "Provider." The international GovTech company providing the software platform and, critically, the expert advice that led to the central procedural failure.

2.3 The North Swanage Traffic Concern Group (NSTCG)

The NSTCG is an informal, self-funded body of local residents and businesses. Our objective is to ensure that community engagement on the Shore Road project is fair, inclusive, and democratically legitimate. We undertook a grassroots campaign to encourage our neighbours to participate in the survey, an action wholly in keeping with the stated goals of the consultation. It was the success of this campaign that precipitated the events detailed below.

Appendix A: Technical Briefing on CAPTCHA Systems: Legitimate Use vs. Misapplication

1.0 Purpose and Intended Use

A CAPTCHA (Completely Automated Public Turing test to tell Computers and Humans Apart) is a security mechanism designed for a single, specific purpose: to mitigate large-scale, automated attacks perpetrated by non-human actors (bots). Its legitimate application is warranted only when there is clear technical evidence of malicious, automated activity, such as botnet-driven spam or Denial-of-Service attacks.

2.0 Technical Signatures of Automated Attacks vs. Legitimate Traffic Spikes

A competent technical analysis must differentiate between malicious automation and genuine human engagement. The technical signatures are distinct and non-ambiguous. The observed "uptick in traffic" was a direct and foreseeable result of a successful, on-the-ground public canvassing campaign. This activity generates traffic matching the profile of a Legitimate Human Traffic Spike.

Metric Malicious Bot Attack Signature Legitimate Human Traffic Spike Signature
IP Address Origin Homogenous; typically from a narrow range of data centre or known proxy IP blocks. Highly diverse; originating from residential ISP and mobile carrier IP blocks within the relevant geographic area (Swanage).
User-Agent String Uniform or limited variation; often identifies headless browsers or scripts (e.g., cURL, Python-requests). Highly diverse; represents a wide variety of browsers, operating systems, and device types used by the general public.
Behavioural Patterns Instantaneous; forms filled in milliseconds. No human-like mouse movement or scrolling. Human-like timing; variable time-on-page, demonstrable scrolling, and form completion time consistent with human reading speed.
Request Rate Extremely high request rates from single IPs or small clusters of IPs. Distributed request rates consistent with individual human activity from separate households.

3.0 Conclusion: Misapplication in the Context of the Swanage Survey

To interpret a predictable increase in community engagement as a malicious attack and deploy a high-friction security response is a fundamental misapplication of the technology. It reveals a critical failure of technical analysis and professional judgment. It treats citizen engagement as a threat to be mitigated, not an objective to be encouraged.

Appendix B: Formal Analysis of Accessibility Compliance Failure (WCAG 2.1 & Equality Act 2010)

1.0 Governing Frameworks

The provision of this digital public service is governed by the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG 2.1) at Level AA and the Equality Act 2010. The implementation of a visual/puzzle-based CAPTCHA represents a significant failure to meet the requirements of both.

2.0 Table of Specific WCAG 2.1 Success Criteria Failures

WCAG Success Criterion Description of Failure by CAPTCHA Implementation
1.1.1 Non-text Content (A) The challenge itself is an image. Standard audio alternatives are notoriously distorted and difficult to understand, thus are not an equivalent alternative.
2.2.1 Timing Adjustable (A) Many systems time-out after failed attempts, penalising users with motor or cognitive disabilities who require more time.
3.3.2 Labels or Instructions (A) Instructions are often ambiguous (e.g., "Select all squares with crosswalks"), causing confusion and repeated failures.
4.1.2 Name, Role, Value (A) The CAPTCHA is often not structured correctly for assistive technologies, leaving screen reader users with no context or ability to interact.

3.0 Breach of Duties under the Equality Act 2010

These technical failures translate directly into breaches of legal duties.

  • Indirect Discrimination (Section 19): The CAPTCHA is a "provision, criterion, or practice" that, while applied to all, puts people with protected characteristics (Age, Disability) at a particular disadvantage.
  • Failure in the Duty to Make Reasonable Adjustments (Section 20): By implementing a known accessibility barrier, the providers had a duty to offer a reasonable adjustment, such as a dedicated phone line or email address for submission. No such adjustment was offered.

4.0 Conclusion

The deployment of the CAPTCHA was not an inconvenient security measure. It was the introduction of a discriminatory practice that fails to meet internationally recognised technical standards and breaches legal duties required by UK law. This action knowingly disenfranchised a significant and protected portion of the target community.

Appendix C: Failure to Adhere to Standard Industry Protocol: The Tiered Threat Response Model

1.0 The Principle of Proportionality

Standard industry protocol for web security dictates a tiered, proportionate response to anomalous traffic. A competent provider does not immediately deploy their most aggressive, user-hostile tool. The objective is to mitigate genuine threats with minimal impact on legitimate users.

2.0 Less Intrusive Security Alternatives

A series of less intrusive, more modern, and more user-respecting security alternatives should have been considered first. The failure to do so demonstrates a lack of professional diligence. These include:

  • Honeypot Fields: Invisible form fields that are ignored by human users but filled out by simple bots. Submissions with these fields filled can be silently discarded.
  • Time-Based Analysis: Measuring the time taken to submit a form. Submissions completed in milliseconds are clearly automated and can be flagged or discarded.
  • IP-Based Rate Limiting: Temporarily blocking submissions from a single IP address that is sending an impossibly high number of requests.
  • Invisible reCAPTCHA (v3): A modern, risk-analysis-based system that scores user interactions in the background without requiring a visual puzzle, only presenting a challenge to the most suspicious traffic.

3.0 Conclusion: A Failure of Professional Diligence

The decision to bypass these standard, less intrusive measures and immediately deploy a universal, high-friction CAPTCHA represents a disproportionate and archaic response. It is a tool of last resort that was used as a first response, demonstrating a critical failure of professional judgment and a disregard for the user experience of the community.

Appendix D: Failure to Meet Lawful Standards of Public Consultation (The Gunning Principles)

Public consultation in the UK is governed by common law principles for fairness, established in R v Brent London Borough Council, ex parte Gunning (1985). The mid-survey implementation of CAPTCHA caused a clear failure to meet at least three of the four principles.

1. Consultation must be at a formative stage.

While the survey began at a formative stage, the unilateral alteration of its access mechanism was not. A fundamental change was introduced after public opinion had already begun to be recorded, invalidating this principle by retrospectively changing the conditions of engagement.

2. The proposer must give sufficient reasons for any proposal to permit of intelligent consideration and response.

This principle extends to the process itself. The public was given no reason or warning for the sudden introduction of a participation barrier. This lack of transparency precludes "intelligent consideration" of the process and fosters distrust.

3. Adequate time must be given for consideration and response.

This is the most egregious failure. The CAPTCHA functionally removes or reduces time for specific demographics. Each failed attempt, each moment of frustration leading to abandonment, is time being stolen from the user. For an older resident, a 5-minute struggle can be the difference between completing the survey and giving up entirely.

4. The product of consultation must be conscientiously taken into account.

The "product" of this consultation is now a contaminated dataset. By implementing a change that systematically biases the results, the consulting body has created a product that cannot be conscientiously taken into account, as it is no longer a valid representation of public opinion.

Conclusion: The actions taken fail the established legal tests for a fair and lawful public consultation. The process has been rendered procedurally improper and is therefore vulnerable to legal challenge.

Appendix F: The Statistical Invalidity of a Contaminated Dataset

1.0 The Principle of a Valid Representative Sample

For a survey's results to be statistically valid, the data must be collected under consistent conditions. A representative sample allows one to make inferences about the entire population. If the method of collection changes part-way through, the sample's integrity is destroyed.

2.0 The Contamination Event

The implementation of the CAPTCHA mid-survey created a clear contamination event. It split the survey's respondents into two incompatible cohorts who were subjected to different conditions:

  • Cohort A (Pre-CAPTCHA): An open, low-friction submission process.
  • Cohort B (Post-CAPTCHA): A closed, high-friction, discriminatory submission process.

3.0 Systematic Bias vs. Random Noise

The procedural change did not merely add random "noise" to the data. It introduced a powerful systematic bias. The CAPTCHA acts as a filter, specifically removing or reducing responses from a particular demographic: those who are older, less tech-savvy, or have visual, motor, or cognitive impairments. As this demographic may hold different views on traffic and infrastructure from other groups, systematically excluding them fundamentally skews the final results.

4.0 Conclusion

The dataset is irrevocably contaminated. It is not possible to merge the two cohorts, nor is it possible to know "what might have been" had the conditions remained consistent. Any attempt to analyse this data as a single, representative sample of the Swanage community would be statistically illiterate and professionally negligent. The data is biased, untrustworthy, and unfit for the purpose of informing public policy.

Potential Weaknesses & Counter-Arguments (Areas to Mitigate)

You must anticipate the defence strategy of your opponents. They will not admit to malice. Here are the most likely attacks and how to prepare for them.

  1. The "Incompetence, Not Conspiracy" Defence.

    • How to Mitigate: Your entire case must be built on the principle that intent is irrelevant; the outcome was discriminatory and the process was unlawful. Proving a conspiracy is hard and unnecessary. Focus relentlessly on the objective, documented failures. Use the phrase: "Whether this was a result of incompetence or intent, the result is the same: a discriminatory and unlawful process that disenfranchised this community."
  2. The "Unrepresentative Activist" Dismissal.

The Petition Campaign: Modern Strategy for Maximum Impact

1. The Petition: How It Should Be Held

You are right to question this. How you hold the petition directly affects its legitimacy and power. You can collect names yourself, but to make it truly effective and legally sound, you must follow specific rules.

The Short Answer: Do not just collect names and emails on a piece of paper or a simple web form. For maximum legal and political weight, use the official UK Government and Parliament Petitions website. Supplement this with local, in-person collection that adheres to GDPR.

Option A: The Gold Standard (Recommended)

  • Platform: The official UK Government and Parliament Petitions website (`petition.parliament.uk`).
  • How it Works: You submit a petition with a clear, specific request for action from the UK Government or House of Commons. For example: "Call for an independent inquiry into the conduct of Swanage Town Council and its supplier, Granicus Inc., regarding the discriminatory and unlawful Swanage Shore Road consultation."
  • Why it's the Best Option:
    • Unquestionable Legitimacy: Signatures are verified. No one can accuse you of faking it.
    • Guaranteed Official Response: If you get 10,000 signatures, the government must formally respond in writing.
    • Potential for a Parliamentary Debate: If you get 100,000 signatures, the petition will be considered for debate in Parliament. This is the ultimate goal for public pressure.
    • Media Power: It gives you a clear, measurable goal that journalists can easily report on ("Campaigners are 1,000 signatures away from forcing a government response...").

Option B: The Local Campaign Standard (Good Alternative/Supplement)

  • Platform: A major, reputable third-party site like 38 Degrees or Change.org.
  • Why it's good: These platforms are well-known, trusted, and make sharing easy. They are excellent for targeting a local body like a council directly. You can aim for a smaller, more achievable number of signatures to demonstrate local support.

Self-Collection (Essential Supplement, but Not as a Standalone)

You absolutely should collect signatures in person at community events or on the street. This reaches people who are not online. However, you MUST do this lawfully:

  • GDPR is Non-Negotiable: When you collect names, emails, and addresses, you are a Data Controller. You must have a clear privacy notice on your clipboard or form. It should state:
    • Who you are (NSTCG).
    • What you are using their data for (to support the petition against STC).
    • Your lawful basis for processing (Consent).
    • That you will store their data securely and delete it after the campaign.
  • Get Explicit Consent: Your form should have a checkbox saying: "I consent for the NSTCG to use my details for the purpose of this campaign and to add my name to the official online petition."

Recommended Hybrid Strategy:

  1. Create the primary petition on the official UK Parliament website. This is your single source of truth.
  2. Create paper-based petition forms for local outreach, with the clear GDPR consent clause.
  3. Manually add the names of people who have given you explicit consent on the paper forms to the online petition, if the platform allows, or at least count them in your public statements (e.g., "We have 2,500 signatures on the official site, plus an additional 500 signatures from local residents collected in person.").
